Why the World Loves Matz

It is easy to admire Yukihiro "Matz" Matsumoto for creating Ruby. That alone
would be enough. Very few people make something that changes how thousands of
other people think, work, learn, and build. But the reason people love Matz
seems to run deeper than technical achievement.

Ruby feels like it was made by someone who remembered that programmers are
people first. It gives you room to breathe. It lets small ideas stay small and
beautiful ideas become readable. In a field that can sometimes make people feel
like they are serving the machine, Matz built a language that quietly insisted
the machine should serve us.

That spirit is why the Ruby community's old motto still feels so tender:
"Matz Is Nice And So We Are Nice." A WIRED article, even while arguing against
Ruby as a modern language choice, pauses to describe Matz as exceptionally kind.
That says something. Even criticism of Ruby often leaves room for affection for
the person who gave it to the world.

The heart of Matz's work is his belief that programming should include joy. The
Ruby language article records his focus on productivity, simplicity, enjoyment,
and happiness. Those words can sound soft until you have spent a long night with
a tool that fights you. Then they start to sound radical. Matz understood that a
language can change a developer's mood, confidence, and sense of possibility.

So the world loves Matz not just because he made Ruby, but because Ruby carries
his kindness forward. Every clear little script, every elegant block, every
moment when code suddenly feels humane instead of hostile, is part of that
legacy. Matz gave programmers more than syntax. He gave them permission to want
their work to feel good.
